Dickey Fuller Test¶

$$ \Delta y_t = a_0 + a_1t + \delta y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t $$
  • $ \epsilon_t $ error term (will be minimized)
  • $\Delta$ first difference operator
  • $a_0$ drift term
  • $a_1$ time trend or deterministic term

for example for $y_t$ in {0, 1, 2 ... 3} the overdetermined system

$$ y_0 = a_0 + a_1 0 + \delta 0 $$$$ y_1-y_0 = a_0 + a_1 1 + \delta y_{0} $$$$ y_2-y_1 = a_0 + a_1 2 + \delta y_{1} $$$$ y_3-y_2 = a_0 + a_1 3 + \delta y_{2} $$

Augmented Dickey Fuller Test¶

replacing $a_0=\alpha$, $a1=\beta$ and $\delta=\gamma$ Wikipedia convention .

$$ \Delta y_t = \alpha + \beta t + \gamma y_{t-1} + \delta_1 \Delta y_{t-1} + ... + \delta_{p-1} \Delta y_{t-p+1} + \epsilon_t $$
  • $ \epsilon_t $ error term (will be minimized)
  • $\Delta$ first difference operator
  • $\alpha$ drift term
  • $\beta$ time trend or deterministic term
  • $p$ lag order must be choosen, using for example the Akaike and Schwartz (Bayesian) information criteria.

for example for $y_t$ in {0, 1, 2 ... 8}, N=9 and p=2. the overdetermined system is:

$$ \Delta y_3 = \alpha + \beta 3 + \gamma y_{2} + \delta_1 \Delta y_2 + \delta_2 \Delta y_1 $$$$ \Delta y_4 = \alpha + \beta 4 + \gamma y_{3} + \delta_1 \Delta y_3 + \delta_2 \Delta y_2 $$$$ \Delta y_5 = \alpha + \beta 5 + \gamma y_{4} + \delta_1 \Delta y_4 + \delta_2 \Delta y_3 $$$$ \Delta y_6 = \alpha + \beta 6 + \gamma y_{5} + \delta_1 \Delta y_5 + \delta_2 \Delta y_4 $$$$ \Delta y_7 = \alpha + \beta 7 + \gamma y_{6} + \delta_1 \Delta y_6 + \delta_2 \Delta y_5 $$$$ \Delta y_8 = \alpha + \beta 8 + \gamma y_{7} + \delta_1 \Delta y_7 + \delta_2 \Delta y_6 $$

  • General case with data points $y_t$ in {0, 1, 2 ... N} and p=p.

  • Variables are $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$ 3 plus p $\delta$'s, total is 3+p.

  • N must be >= 3+p otherwise cannot apply ordinary least squares
$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & p+1 & y_{p} & \Delta y_{p} & \Delta y_{p-1}& \cdots & \Delta y_{1} \\ 1 & p+2 & y_{p+1} & \Delta y_{p+1} & \Delta y_{p} & \cdots & \Delta y_{2} \\ 1 & p+3 & y_{p+2} & \Delta y_{p+2} & \Delta y_{p+1}& \cdots & \Delta y_{3} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 1 & N-1 & y_{N-2} & \Delta y_{N-2} & \Delta y_{N-3}& \cdots & \Delta y_{N-p-1} \\ \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \\ \gamma \\ \delta_1 \\ \delta_2 \\ \vdots \\ \delta_p \\ \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \Delta y_{p+1}\\ \Delta y_{p+2}\\ \Delta y_{p+3}\\ \vdots \\ \Delta y_{N-1}\\ \end{pmatrix} $$
$$ \mathbf{X}_{N-p-1,3+p} \cdot \mathbf{\beta}_{3+p} = \Delta y_{N-p-1} $$

that is a overdermined last squares problem, using $z = \Delta y$:

$$ \mathbf{X} \cdot \mathbf{\beta} = z $$

The last squares solution is, :

$$ \mathbf{\hat{\beta}} = \left(\mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}^T z $$

The error

$$ \mathbf{\hat{\epsilon}} = z - \mathbf{X} \mathbf{\hat{\beta}}$$

The reduced chi-squared statistic:

$$ s^2 = \frac{\mathbf{\hat{\epsilon}}^T \mathbf{\hat{\epsilon}}}{N-p-1-(p+3)} $$

If the strict exogeneity does not hold (as is the case with many time series models, where exogeneity is assumed only with respect to the past shocks but not the future ones), then these estimators will be biased in finite samples. The estimated standard error of each coeficient $\mathbf{\hat{\beta}}_j$ is

$$ \hat{\sigma}_j = \hat{s.e.}\left(\mathbf{\hat{\beta}}_j \right) = \sqrt{s^2 \left(\mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X}\right)^{-1}_{jj}} $$

For the specific case of $\mathbf{\hat{\beta}}_3$ ~ $\gamma$

$$ \hat{\sigma}_3 = \hat{s.e.}\left(\mathbf{\hat{\gamma}} \right) = \sqrt{s^2 \left(\mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X}\right)^{-1}_{33}} $$

The the test statistic for $\mathbf{\hat{\gamma}}$ is

$$ t = \frac{\mathbf{\hat{\gamma}}}{\hat{\sigma}_3} $$

Exact Solution for $ADF$ or batch size $ADF's$ (equal size)¶

  • Implemented in pytorch_adf fully test against statsmodel

Aproximated Solution for $SADF_t$ - batch size $ADF's$ (different sizes)¶

  • for a $SADF_t$ $t$ point
  • Start with matrix of the OLS of backward window of maximum length call it $X$

  • For each $ADF$ required smaller than the previous zero the number of corresponding lines on $X$

  • Implemented on pytorch_sadft with numerical errors

Numerical errors of aproximation for calculating thousands of ADF's at once on GPU/CPU¶

  • Aproximations is : zeroing first i lines of $X$ when doing $(X^T \cdot X)^{-1}$ for doing each smaller $ADF$ in $SADF_t$

    • using LU decomposition torch.inverse

  • That's Greatly reduced by using cholesky_inverse| cholesky_solve for symmetric positive definite matrixes $X^T \cdot X$ case

  • other possibilities to test (don't know if they would be usefull for something)
    • torch.qr QR factorization where Q is orthogonal and R upper triangular
      • very commom for OLS solution
    • torch.lstsq computes the solution to the least squares problem

The Gramian Matrix $X^T\cdot X$

Considering $X_1, X_2 \cdots X_{p} $ colum vectors of Matrix $X$ the Gramian Matrix of $X$ is defined as the matrix of every dot product of these vectors.

$G_{ij} = X_i\cdot X_j $ Eistein notation

X = th.rand(x0, p, dtype=th.float32)

for i in range(x0-p):

    a = th.matmul(Xs.transpose(0, -1).view(1, p, x0-i), Xs.view(1, x0-i, p)).view(p, -1)

    b = th.cholesky_solve(th.eye(p), L, False).sum()

Min window minw too small problems¶

  • OLS matrix $X^T\cdot X$ can become bad conditioned - (Cholesky will fail!)

    • if data is too similar on the previous times (lines will be almost linear dependent!)

  • OLS number of lines (data size) too close to number of parameters being estimated

    • test statistics controlled by 2*p+4

The less data (smaller minw) you use the more probable that's going to happen¶

X = th.zeros((n-p-1, 3+p), device=dev, dtype=th.float32)

diffilter = th.tensor([-1., 1.], device=dev, dtype=th.float32).view(1, 1, 2)
dy = th.conv1d(y.view(1, 1, -1), diffilter).view(-1)
z = dy[p:].clone()
# X matrix
X[:, 0] = 1 # drift
X[:, 1] = th.arange(p+1, n) # deterministic trend
X[:, 2] = y[p:-1]# regression data (nobs)
# fill in columns, max lagged serial correlations

    X[:, 2+i] = dy[p-i:-i]
# sadf loop until minw, every matrix is smaller than the previous
# zeroing i lines, observations are becomming less also
batch_size = maxw-minw
Xb = X.repeat(batch_size, 1).view(batch_size, X.shape[0], X.shape[1])
zb = z.repeat(batch_size, 1).view(batch_size, X.shape[0], 1) # additonal dim for matrix*vector mult.
nobsi = th.zeros(batch_size, dtype=th.float32)

ststats = np.zeros(batch_size)
#zeromask = th.ones(batch_size, nobs, dtype=th.float32)
for i in range(batch_size): # each is smaller than previous
    Xb[i, :i, :] = 0
    zb[i, :i] = 0
    nobsi[i] = n-(float(i)+2*p+4)
    sXb = X[i:, :]
    szb = z[i:]
    sXt = sXb.t()
